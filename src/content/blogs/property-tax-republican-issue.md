If you own a home in Bastrop County, you already know the feeling. You open your appraisal notice, do the math, and wonder how the number went up *again*. You're not imagining it. Bastrop County has been one of the fastest-growing counties in Texas for the better part of a decade, and growth has consequences — not all of them good.

Let's cut through the noise and talk about what's actually happening with your property tax bill, who's responsible, and what the Republican Party believes should be done about it.

### The Anatomy of Your Tax Bill

Your property tax bill isn't one tax — it's several. Your total bill is split between multiple taxing entities:

- **Bastrop County** (roads, law enforcement, courts)
- **Your school district** (the single largest portion)
- **Special districts** (ESD, water, etc.)
- **City taxes** (if you live inside city limits)

Each entity sets its own tax rate, and each one gets a slice of whatever your property is appraised at. When your appraisal goes up 15% and no one lowers their rate, your bill goes up 15%. It's that simple — and that frustrating.

### What Republicans Stand For

The Republican Party of Texas platform is clear: **government should live within its means, not yours.** We believe in:

- **Lower tax rates** that reflect actual service needs, not wishful spending
- **Transparent budgeting** where every dollar is justified to the taxpayer
- **Appraisal reform** that prevents runaway valuations from pricing families out of their own homes
- **Holding the line** on new spending that isn't tied to essential services

At the county level, Commissioners Court sets the county tax rate every year. That vote matters enormously — and it's one of the most undercovered decisions in local government.

### What You Can Do

1. **Attend budget hearings.** Commissioners Court holds public hearings before setting the tax rate each year. Show up. Your presence matters.
2. **Protest your appraisal.** The Bastrop Central Appraisal District allows you to formally challenge your property valuation. The deadline is usually May 15, but informal reviews happen year-round.
3. **Know your elected officials.** Visit our [Elected Officials page](https://www.bastropctygop.com/resources/elected-officials/) to find out who represents you at every level.
4. **Vote in local elections.** Turnout in county races is often shockingly low. Your vote carries real weight.

### The Bottom Line

Property taxes aren't an abstract policy debate in Bastrop County — they're the difference between a family staying in their home and being priced out. Republicans believe that hardworking Texans should keep more of what they earn, and that government at every level should be held accountable for every dollar it spends.
