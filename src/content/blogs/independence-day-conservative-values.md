Every year on the Fourth of July, we gather with our families, fire up the grill, watch the fireworks, and celebrate the birthday of the greatest nation in the history of the world. Here in Bastrop County, we do it with Texas-sized pride — parades through Smithville, flags lining Main Street, and neighbors who still know each other by name.

But beneath the celebration is something deeper. Independence Day isn't just a holiday — it's a reminder of the ideas that made America possible. And those ideas are, at their core, conservative ideas.

### The Founding Was Conservative

The Declaration of Independence wasn't a revolutionary manifesto in the way we usually think about revolutions. It wasn't a call to tear down every institution and start over. It was a carefully reasoned argument that certain truths are **self-evident** — that rights come from God, not from government, and that when government violates those rights, the people have the authority to change course.

That's conservatism in its purest form:

- **Individual liberty** over collective control
- **Limited government** that serves the people, not the other way around
- **God-given rights** that no legislature can grant or revoke
- **Self-governance** rooted in the consent of the governed

The Founders didn't trust power. They designed a system with checks, balances, and a Bill of Rights specifically because they understood that power corrupts — and that the rights of ordinary citizens must be protected against the ambitions of those who govern.

### What This Means Today

The principles of 1776 aren't historical curiosities. They're the foundation of every policy debate we engage in today:

- **When we fight for the Second Amendment**, we're defending the Founders' belief that a free people must have the means to protect themselves.
- **When we push back against government overreach**, we're honoring the same instinct that led colonists to resist a distant king who taxed them without representation.
- **When we insist on fiscal responsibility**, we're following the principle that government should do only what is necessary — and nothing more.
- **When we defend parental rights in education**, we're affirming that families, not the state, are the primary unit of society.

These aren't partisan talking points. They're American principles that have been defended by every generation since the founding — and they need defending again today.

### A Final Thought

John Adams, writing to his wife Abigail on July 3, 1776 — the day before independence was declared — said that the occasion should be celebrated with *"pomp and parade, with shows, games, sports, guns, bells, bonfires, and illuminations, from one end of this continent to the other, from this time forward, forevermore."*

Two hundred and fifty years later, we're still doing exactly that. And in Bastrop County, we're doing it with the same spirit of liberty, gratitude, and resolve that the Founders intended.

Happy Independence Day, Bastrop County. God bless Texas, and God bless the United States of America.
